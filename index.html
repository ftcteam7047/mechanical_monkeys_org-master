About

Let us introduce ourselves.

 

FTC Team

FIRST® Tech Challenge (FTC) is one of the levels from the FIRST® robotics program. Students design, build, program, fundraise, and drive robots for competitions. Teams then compete head on in tournaments that advance all the way to a global final. We are proud to be an FTC Team.

Educators

A mission of FIRST is to spread STEM to people everywhere. Our team fulfills this through our outreach. We have presented at exhibitions, assisted at other schools, run events, and more. During our off-season, we plan to continue to spread FIRST.

Learners

Through the FTC program, we get a hands-on experience with programming, building, designing, teamwork, and so much more. This is a unique opportunity specific to STEM (science, technology, engineering, math) which helps foster youth to have a better understanding of STEM jobs, with the intent to help them go into this ever-expanding field.

    Journey

    From the drawing board to the competition.

    • September 9

      The Kickoff

      Our team participated at the first Bloomfield Hills High School FTC Kickoff. The day before, many of our team members helped set-up the event. Here, we took classes and demonstrated our robot to other teams. As a team, we watched this year's game challenge video- Relic Recovery. Immediately, we started strategizing and discussing the game.

    • September 9- October 7

      Designing and Building

      In the meetings that followed, we continued to strategize and then design. Everyone contributed ideas which led to a prototyping session. Finally, we started to build our robot with input from our mechanical mentor, George. All builders were assigned a different part of the robot to build.

    • October 7- November 10

      Programming and Testing

      Our programming team worked with the build team to test out the robot. After the building was completed, and the programming was ready for the drivers. As the drivers tested the robot, they found little things they wanted to adjust to make driving easier for them. The programmers then adjusted the programs accordingly.

    • November 11

      The Mason Qualifier

      This year, at the Mason Qualifer, we won all 5 qualifer matches, which helped to put us in 1st place. Because of this, we were the captain of Alliance #1. Our alliance partners were the CSPA Miners and the Black Frogs. After 5 challenging matches, Alliance #1 came out as the winning alliance of the Mason Qualifer. We also set the highest match score up to that date for Relic Recovery with the CSPA Miners. Our team will compete at Battle Creek at the FTC State Competition this December.

    • November 12- December 1

      Improvements

      Our team discussed what we saw at the Mason Competition. After a team meeting, we decided to build a second robot that needed to be ready for states. This second robot would be a total redesign from the first robot as we needed our robot to be faster than it could be. Our team also began to work in Outreach. We made general improvements to our robot for the upcoming Kettering Qualifier.

    • December 2

      The Kettering Qualifier

      This year, at the Kettering Qualifer, we placed 17th in the robot game. While the results weren't what we hoped they would be, our team went on to win the Control Award, become the finalists for the Rockwell Collins Innovate Award and Think Award, and win 3rd Place Inspire.

    • December 3- December 14

      Improvements and Outreach

      The Mechanical Monkeys continue to build the second robot. Meanwhile, they also film and create the Compass and Promote videos. Our team visited Bridge Academy West's FTC team, and helped them fix their robot just in time for their competition on December 9! We also arranged two Hour of STEM events to be held at Way Elementary for the 4th grade.

    • December 15- December 16

      FIRST in Michigan FTC Championships

      AT FIM FTC Championships, our team was the captain of alliance #7 in the elimination rounds on Edison. We won the Control Award, became the runner-up for the Motivate Award, and became the runner up for the Inspire Award. Because of this, we advanced to the North Super Regional. We are honored to have won these achievements.

    • December 17- March 13

      Improvements and Outreach

      Our team rewrote a new presentation. We ran more Hour of STEM Events, went to a Metro Parent Event, and presented at Hazel Park Junior High's STEM Night to help them start their own team. We made significant improvements to our robot that would help us prevent jams and make our robot more efficient. We also trained a neural network that could detect glyphs as a team.

    • March 15- March 17

      FTC North Super Regionals

      From March 15 to March 17, we competed against 72 teams from eleven other states in Cedar Rapids, Iowa. The Mechanical Monkeys ranked number three in our division after the qualifying rounds. We were the only Michigan team to become one of the eight alliance captains during the elimination rounds. Additionally, we were finalists for both the Control Award and the Promote Award. Our high standing in the qualifying rounds has helped our team advance to the 2018 FIRST FTC World Championships. We are honored to be one of the four teams from Michigan to advance the World Championships. The Mechanical Monkeys are the first FTC team in the Bloomfield Hills District to advance to the world level.

    Cost

    Learn about why we need to fundraise.

    While FTC is a really great opportunity to learn, the cost to build a robot can add up very quickly. Components for the robot include motors, sensors, C-Channels, servos, and much, much more. A set of 4 inch Mechanum Wheels costs $199.00. The Rev Robotics Expansion hub is $175.00. Motors and servos cost about $30.00 each, and there are already eight on the robot. The rest of the cost includes competition registration fees, marketing materials, food, and more. The average team will spend $7000+ in one season. That's why we need your help so we can journey forward in our mission to spread and learn STEM.

    Our Robot

    Meet Curious George.


    Curious George 2.0

    Curious George 1.0

    Our robot, Curious George, has gone through many changes over the journey of designing it, building it, improving it, and fixing it. After our 1st competition, Curious George 1.0 helped us scored the highest North Region match score (and at the time, the highest score in the country! As of 12/11/17, it still holds as the top North Region Match score!). However, with so many ideas and room for improvement, we began to deisgn a 2nd robot. The result was Curious George 2.0.

    Our robot pulls glyphs in with an intake system. The primary intake system is powered by one DC motor. After a block is pulled in, they are guided along by silicone green wheels, the secondary intake system. It lifts the block onto the ramp inside. The ramp can hold two glyphs at a time. For the lower two rows, after it collects one or two glyphs, a servo in the back of the robot flips the glyphs up, into the cryptobox. For the top two rows, the servo aligns the ramp parallel with the ground and a dc motor raises a lift that allows the robot to place glyphs into the cryptobox. Gravity then can bring the glyphs down.

    Our first chassis didn’t look the way it does now. In fact, we went through three chassis to get to the current one with the mecanum wheels. In our first chassis, we had 3 wheels on each side in a chained into a triangle formation, with two wheels as omni wheels, and the other just a regular wheel. However, the robot would sway when it drove, making it really unstable. So, we reduced it to two wheels chained up to each other on each side, and changed the wheels into stealth wheels. However, the stealth wheels caused too much friction so the robot couldn’t turn. We switched the wheels into omni wheels, but it took too long to align the glyphs with the columns. So, we switched to mecanum wheels which allowed us to move sideways and diagonally. That helped us efficiently place glyphs into the cryptobox.

    The first robot had a drawer slide lift that was raised using a DC motor, a pulley system, and a servo. 4 servos on the lift clamp onto two glyphs at a time. A ball arm lowered during automomous between the two jewels to remove the opponent's ball. There is a relic arm that folds out in two stages. A claw at the very end has foam acting as a gripper that helps grab onto the relic. The relic arm was never used due to strategic decisions. We used mecanum wheels on our robot.

    In our original glyph-lift, instead of a chain, we used fishing-line. However, we learned that the fishing line could tangle. So, we switched to the chain, which couldn't tangle.

    The Programming Team

    Michael, Om, Bowie

    In FTC, teams need to program their robot so they can control it during the driver-control period, and have the robot perform various tasks with pre-programmed instructions. Students who choose to program use Java. Our programming team consists of two veteran FTC programmers. This year, our programming team is using Google Brain's TensorFlow so they can use a neural network to identify the game's "jewels". Using this, the robot will be able to figure out which "jewel" to remove. Computer Neural Networks are systems based off of biological neurons, that are trained to complete certain tasks without task-specific programming.

    Helping the teams around us.

    On December 7, our team visited the Bridge Academy West in Detroit. We met with their school's own FTC team, Team 8753, The Eagles.     When the presentation was finished, we were shown Team 8753's robot. They had a competition on the 9th at Dearborn. Howevever, they were having problems with their robot as their servos for their glyph-grabber weren't functioning, and their glyph-arm could not raise or lower. We worked with their programmers to teach them how to fix their program. Our builders also found a few parts in their mechanical design that we helped clean up a bit. At the end, we helped get their robot working, and we all had a really great learning experience. On December 9, The Eagles went on to win 4 of their matches! We plan to help mentor their team in the future.

    Way Elementary, "Hour of STEM" Event

    An event to inspire.

    Our team mate, Heather, created a plan for a three-activity, hour long, "Hour of STEM" event. Then, after the approval of some mentors, she contacted Way Elementary School's principal. Thus, the "Hour of STEM" event was created. On December 11, the Mechanical Monkeys went to Way, and ran the event with half of the 4th grade. Three groups of students rotated through three STEM-related activities. They learned about acid-base chemical reactions with a fun exploding-lunchbag experiment. Students had the chance to drive the Mechanical Monkeys robot too! They also got to explore the connection between math and programming with a "rotation of wheels" activity that taught wheel circumference and how it can be used to find how many times a wheel needs to rotate to go a certain distance. The Mechanical Monkeys will be returning to Way on December 18 to run their second "Hour of STEM" event. They hope that this event will inspire these students to join robotics, or learn more about STEM topics.
